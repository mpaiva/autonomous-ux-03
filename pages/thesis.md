## The one-line thesis

**Penumbra is a top-tier AI product design studio whose work is inclusive by
default.** Accessibility is not a service line bolted onto the side — it is the
proof of our quality, rigor, trust, and enterprise readiness.

We deliberately do **not** lead as "an accessibility studio." That framing caps
pricing, narrows perceived scope, and invites the perception of a compliance
vendor. We lead with AI product design craft; accessibility is the evidence that
the craft is real.

> The name *Penumbra* — the graded region between full light and full shadow —
> stands for the idea at the center of inclusive design: design for the full
> spectrum of human ability, not just the brightly-lit center. Designing for the
> edges makes the product better for everyone (the curb-cut effect).

## The wedge: Accessible AI UX

This is the niche where **AI interaction design meets inclusive design**. We
evaluated it against five tests:

| Test | Verdict | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Narrow enough to be defensible? | **Conditional** | The position is defensible *only* with a proprietary moat. As taste-plus-services it is absorbable. |
| Underserved enough? | **Yes** | No commercial studio owns the blend. AI studios ignore disability; accessibility firms treat AI as a thing to audit, not a medium to design. |
| Valuable to buyers? | **Yes** | Regulatory urgency + litigation + procurement gates create budget and deadlines. |
| Credible for us to own? | **Yes, if authentic** | Only if the work is genuinely tested and disability-led. See the [credibility model]({{ '/credibility/' | relative_url }}). |
| Differentiated enough for premium pricing? | **Yes** | The AI-interface accessibility problem is a *design-craft* problem that commodity auditors and overlays cannot solve. |

The sharp, defensible craft niche inside the wedge: **accessible patterns for
generative, streaming, agentic, and voice interfaces** — focus management on
streamed tokens, per-message screen-reader navigation, "an agent is acting on
your behalf" states, voice/text parity, and AI-disclosure UX. The leading
design-thought sources name "trust" as the central AI design problem of 2026 but
do not even mention accessibility. That silence is our opening.

## The three strategic options — how we decided to use them

### 1. Accessible AI UX as the primary wedge — ADOPTED
We lead with it, but framed as craft and proof of quality, not as compliance.
See [DR-0001]({{ '/decisions/positioning-wedge/' | relative_url }}).

### 2. Accessibility as recurring revenue — ADOPTED, as the annuity
The money compounds *after* the one-time audit: embedded design reviews,
per-release VPAT/ACR refreshes (an ACR is only valid for the version it tested),
monitoring, and design-system maintenance. We optimize for these. See
[Services]({{ '/services/' | relative_url }}) and
[DR-0003]({{ '/decisions/service-ladder/' | relative_url }}).

### 3. Reusable IP as the moat — ADOPTED, as the top priority
Underserved is not the same as defensible. We build proprietary IP first — the
Accessible AI Pattern Library and an evaluation scorecard — so the position is
ownable before incumbents notice the gap. See
[IP roadmap]({{ '/ip-roadmap/' | relative_url }}) and
[DR-0004]({{ '/decisions/ip-first-moat/' | relative_url }}).

## What would falsify this thesis

We hold this as a hypothesis, not a conviction. We would revise direction if:

- An incumbent (an AI studio adding accessibility, or Fable/AbilityNet moving
  upstream into design) credibly claims the blend before we build the moat.
- Buyers treat AI-interface accessibility as "nice to have" rather than a
  procurement or trust requirement once we test willingness to pay.
- We cannot recruit the human accessibility expertise and disability-led
  co-designers required to deliver authentically — in which case the honest move
  is to narrow the claim, not to fake it.

The evidence behind all of this lives in [Research]({{ '/research/' | relative_url }}),
with confidence levels and honest gaps flagged.
